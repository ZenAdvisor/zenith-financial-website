General Queries

A tax deduction is an amount that you can subtract from your taxable income to reduce the total amount of income that is subject to tax. Common deductions include contributions to retirement accounts, mortgage interest, and charitable donations.

A tax credit is an amount that you can subtract directly from the taxes you owe. Unlike deductions, which reduce taxable income, tax credits reduce the amount of tax owed on a dollar-for-dollar basis.

A tax deduction reduces your taxable income, which in turn reduces the amount of tax you owe. A tax credit reduces the amount of tax you owe on a dollar-for-dollar basis. Tax credits are generally more valuable than tax deductions of the same amount.

Filing requirements vary based on your income, filing status, age, and other factors. Generally, you must file a tax return if your income is above a certain threshold. Consult the IRS or CRA guidelines or a tax professional for specific filing requirements.

Canada Taxation FAQs

Canadian residents who own specified foreign property with a total cost of more than CAD 100,000 must file Form T1135. This includes individuals, corporations, trusts, and partnerships.

Includes funds or intangible property situated, deposited, or held outside Canada. Tangible property located outside Canada, shares of non-resident corporations, foreign rental property, and other foreign investments. It does not include property used in an active business, personal-use property, or certain types of registered accounts (e.g., RRSPs, RRIFs).

Description of the property, country in which the property is located, maximum cost amount during the year, cost amount at year-end, income generated from the property, any gains or losses from the disposition of the property.

The form must be filed by the same deadline as the taxpayer's income tax return. For individuals, this is usually April 30 of the following year.

There are significant penalties for failing to file Form T1135 on time. The standard penalty is CAD 25 per day up to a maximum of CAD 2,500 per year. More severe penalties may apply in cases of gross negligence or repeated non-compliance.

Taxpayers with specified foreign property costing between CAD 100,000 and CAD 250,000 can use a simplified reporting method.

Determining residency status for tax purposes can be complex. It is based on the individual's ties to Canada, such as a home, spouse or common-law partner, and dependants in Canada. The CRA also considers secondary ties, such as personal property in Canada, social ties, economic ties, and other relevant factors.

Residents of Canada must report their worldwide income on their Canadian tax returns. This includes income from employment, business, investments, and other sources, regardless of where it was earned.

Individuals who leave Canada and sever their residential ties may be considered non-residents for tax purposes. This affects how their income is taxed and what they must report to the CRA.

Non-residents are taxed on their Canadian-source income, which may include income from employment in Canada, business income earned in Canada, and capital gains from the disposition of certain Canadian property.

Non-residents earning rental income from real property in Canada can choose to file a Section 216 return. This allows them to pay tax on their net rental income instead of having 25% tax withheld on the gross rental income.

The return is due by June 30 of the year following the rental income year.

Filing a Section 216 return can result in a refund if the 25% tax withheld exceeds the tax payable on net rental income.

Foreign owners of residential property in Canada must file an annual UHT return and may be subject to the tax unless they meet certain exemptions. The tax applies to vacant or underused homes owned by non-resident, non-Canadian owners.

Exemptions include properties used as primary residences, properties rented out for at least six months of the year, and properties owned by Canadian citizens or permanent residents.

The UHT is 1% of the property’s assessed value or its most recent sale price, whichever is higher.
